Significant Figures Canadian Black History Month

Viola Desmond

Viola Desmond, born on July 6, 1914, in Halifax, Nova Scotia, was a trailblazing Black Canadian entrepreneur and civil rights activist. She defied racial segregation in 1946 when she refused to leave the whites-only section of a movie theatre, leading to her arrest and subsequent legal battle. Desmond's courageous stand against injustice sparked nationwide attention and became a symbol of resistance against discrimination in Canada. Her legacy as a pioneer in the fight for racial equality was solidified when she became the first Black Canadian woman to appear on the nation's $10 bill in 2018, cementing her enduring impact on Canadian history and inspiring generations to come.

Lincoln Alexander

Lincoln Alexander, born on January 21, 1922, in Toronto, Ontario, was a pioneering figure in Canadian politics and a trailblazer for Black Canadians. He made history in 1968 by becoming the first Black Member of Parliament elected to the House of Commons, representing the riding of Hamilton West. Alexander's political career was marked by his dedication to social justice and his advocacy for marginalized communities. In 1985, he achieved another historic milestone when he was appointed as the Lieutenant Governor of Ontario, becoming the first Black person to hold a vice-regal position in Canada. Throughout his life, Alexander remained a steadfast advocate for diversity, inclusion, and equal opportunity, leaving behind a lasting legacy of leadership and service that continues to inspire Canadians today.

Rosemary Brown

Rosemary Brown, born on June 17, 1930, in Kingston, Jamaica, was a prominent Canadian politician and social activist who made significant contributions to advancing gender equality and racial justice. After immigrating to Canada in 1951, Brown became deeply involved in social activism, advocating for women's rights and racial equality. In 1972, she made history by becoming the first Black woman elected to a Canadian provincial legislature, serving as a Member of the Legislative Assembly of British Columbia. Throughout her political career, Brown was a tireless champion for marginalized communities, fighting against discrimination and working to create a more inclusive society. Her legacy as a pioneering feminist and anti-racism advocate continues to inspire generations of Canadians to pursue social change and equality for all. Jean Augustine

Jean Augustine, born on September 9, 1937, in Grenada, is a trailblazing Canadian politician and educator, renowned for her significant contributions to Canadian society. During her tenure as a Member of Parliament, she championed numerous social justice causes, including advocating for women's rights, immigrant rights, and racial equality. Augustine's most enduring legacy is her instrumental role in establishing Black History Month in Canada. In 1995, she introduced a motion to recognize February as Black History Month, which was unanimously adopted by the House of Commons. Through her leadership and activism, Augustine has left an indelible mark on Canadian politics and society, paving the way for greater diversity, inclusivity, and recognition of Black Canadians' contributions to the nation's rich tapestry.

Portia White

Portia White, born on June 24, 1911, in Truro, Nova Scotia, was a renowned Canadian opera singer and a trailblazer in the world of classical music. Despite facing racial discrimination and limited opportunities due to her Black heritage, White's exceptional vocal talents propelled her to international acclaim. She began her musical journey at an early age, studying piano and voice at the Halifax Conservatory of Music. In 1941, White made history as the first Black Canadian concert singer to achieve widespread recognition on the international stage. Her powerful contralto voice captivated audiences across North America and Europe, earning her accolades and critical acclaim. Throughout her career, White shattered racial barriers in the classical music world and paved the way for future generations of Black artists. Her legacy as a pioneering vocalist and cultural ambassador continues to inspire and uplift aspiring musicians, leaving an indelible mark on Canada's cultural landscape.
